Mario Schlosser, co-founder of Oscar Health, shares the three key principles that enabled him to build a multi-billion dollar company: meticulous time tracking for data-driven decisions, radical decomposition to tackle complex problems, and an unwavering commitment to transparency, even in the face of internal conflict. He recounts personal struggles with mental health and a previous company's failure due to internal strife, highlighting how these experiences shaped his leadership approach at Oscar.

**Topics:** time tracking, data-driven decisions, radical decomposition, company building, health insurance, mental health, internal conflict, transparency, leadership, startup growth, entrepreneurship, venture capital

## Radical Decomposition: Breaking Down Complex Problems [04:15]

**Alexa:** Well, he leaned on something that he learned from Ray Dalio while he was working at Bridgewater, an idea called radical decomposition. And it's how you take an impossibly complex problem and break it down into parts that you can actually solve.

**Mario Schlosser:** So I was at Bridgewater for a couple of years, big macro hedge funds and the best idea underpinning in all of Bridgewater in my view is this idea of, I'm not sure if they call it that way, I call it that way, radical decomposition. You you say, I want to know what inflation will be next year. How do you do that? Well, the government tells you how they report inflation and you can just look up on the websites of the government, of the BEA or BLS, whatever, how they report inflation and the 200 categories that go into the inflation basket in the US. And you can then go through the 200 categories and you can have an estimate for each one of the categories and yeah, those up and you get probably some estimate of inflation going forward. And so, so you take a some complex piece, you break it down to its constituents and then you go one by one, basically.

## Applying Radical Decomposition to Oscar Health [05:11]

**Mario Schlosser:** And so for building Oscar as an insurance company, that list of constituents was the list of components that the that we had to go and operate. Again, claims system, network, all these things. Over the summer, I found a couple of documents, filings from insurance companies, public filings that that you can download from regulatory offices across the across the country, where certain think tanks writing about the state of health insurance in New York State, whatever, stuff like that. And those reports showed me the components of what an insurance company has to have. Again, claims system, network of physicians, underwriting, utilization management, all these things.
