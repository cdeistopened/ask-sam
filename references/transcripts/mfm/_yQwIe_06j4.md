## Who Is Alex Hormozi? [00:00:00]

**Sam:** I've been getting YouTube-recommended this guy Alex Hormozi everywhere. He looks like an 80s cop had a baby with Arnold Schwarzenegger — mustache, huge, jacked. And he's saying: learn from me, a guy running nearly $100 million in revenue. He's 31 years old. I think he's legit, but it's almost too good to be true.

**Sam:** Here's the stack:

**Sam:** He starts as a gym owner at 22-23. Scales to three gyms. Meets people at ClickFunnels and learns copywriting. One of them tells him: your gym is good, but the best gym in the world still isn't that big of a business. Teach gym owners how to do what you did. That becomes **Gym Launch** — a course/mastermind that runs $16,000-$20,000. It's not a passive course, it's more of a held-hand program. Gym owners learn to acquire, serve, and retain customers.

**Sam:** From Gym Launch, he notices gym owners need supplements to sell to their members, so he builds **Prestige Lab** — a supplement company where gym owners can buy and resell under their own brand.

**Sam:** Then he notices gym owners need leads, so he builds a **lead gen company** — essentially, if someone searches "gym near me in San Jose," they hit a landing page, enter their zip code, give their email, and the gym owner gets that lead and pays per contact.

**Sam:** Those three businesses combined were doing $70-80 million per year. He sold 66% of Gym Launch plus the supplement business for $46 million. He said in one of his talks that over five years — between the sale and dividends — he paid himself $72 million.

**Shaan:** That's wild. He's 31.

**Sam:** Then there's **Acquisition.com** — his and his wife's holding company that promises to scale 100 service businesses doing $1-10M in EBITDA over five years without taking a majority interest. He's also bought at least one business outright — Enchanted Fairies, a photography business.

## Is He Legit? [00:07:00]

**Sam:** Here's why I think he's legit: his actual tactical advice is good. One example from a TikTok: instead of asking a prospect "would you like to sign up?", he asks "would you be opposed to signing up if we worked out the details?" People will say "no, I'm not opposed to that" much more easily than "yes." It's a no-commitment framing that moves the conversation forward. I've accidentally done this and it works.

**Shaan:** That's real sales psychology. Not motivational-poster stuff.

**Sam:** My take: he's legit, he's clearly smart, and he seems like a good guy. He's also operating in a world with a lot of shady players — supplements, $20K courses, the clickfunnels orbit. The industry has a stigma because there are a lot of people in it who are shady. That doesn't make him one of them. But it's worth being aware of.

**Shaan:** The "you couldn't do it yourself that's why you're selling courses" criticism — I don't buy it. If you've built real businesses and now you're teaching the playbook, that's a legitimate thing to sell. The question is whether the playbook is actually good.

**Sam:** His playbook seems to be good. I'm not going to give someone $20K to find out for certain, but what he's putting out publicly passes the sniff test.
