## Introduction: LearnVest Origin [00:00:00]

**Sam:** You were the CEO of LearnVest. Give us the two-to-three-minute background.

**Alexa:** I grew up in Jacksonville, Florida. Always been an entrepreneur. Went to Harvard undergrad, studied psychology and happiness research. Then Harvard Business School — dropped out December 18th, 2008 — right into the worst recession in memory. I built a financial planning software company called LearnVest with a simple mission: every American family should be able to get access to a financial plan and a trusted advisor at an affordable price. On our fifth birthday — March 25th, 2015 — we sold to Northwestern Mutual for about $375 million. I stayed for a few years as part of that business, wrote a couple of books, and now I run a $200 million venture fund called Inspired Capital, headquartered in New York City. Married, three kids under four. Completely crazy, but that's who I am.

**Shaan:** I'll start, because Sam's still processing "Harvard, sold for $375 million, financial planning."

**Sam:** I did not go to Harvard. I have an odd fascination with it. I do own a Harvard sweatshirt and people always assume.

## Happiness Research and Enthusiasm [00:06:00]

**Shaan:** You said you studied happiness. What was that like?

**Alexa:** I was in the mind, brain, behavior track and ended up in psychology. Dan Gilbert was running a happiness lab. The headline of the whole thing was: we are really bad at making decisions that make us happy. We think getting the fancy car will make us happy — but then you have to clean the car. We think moving farther out of the city will make us happy — but then you commute further. It's the little things that actually matter.

**Shaan:** Was Shawn Achor one of your teachers?

**Alexa:** Yes — Shawn Achor, Tal Ben-Shahar, Philip Stone. They were all in that world. The core insight is: if you're happy, you'll outperform in the long run. In the short term, you can push through stress. But for building teams over years, you want people who are deeply fulfilled.

**Sam:** I've always thought enthusiasm is in serious undersupply among successful people. Most environments I've been in — entrepreneurs, investors, big company executives — people treat enthusiasm as low status. Being reserved feels more prestigious. I've gone the opposite direction. I think I have one-tenth the skills of most people I've worked with, but enthusiasm has been a huge part of what's worked for me.

**Alexa:** And it's a skill. You can develop it. I can't grow taller, but I can try to be more joyful.

**Shaan:** I would say Sam has an angry enthusiasm. He flips from deeply enthusiastic and infectious — to suddenly very grumpy — then back to enthusiastic, seamlessly. The people around him are just getting rocked.

**Sam:** I will fight with my executives and then it's like: can we just hug it out? We're good.

## Building LearnVest: Content First, Products Second [00:14:00]

**Sam:** I used LearnVest when it first came out. Walk me through the build. It seemed like it pivoted a few times?

**Alexa:** The business plan was always the same: content, tools, and advice. We started with content in May 2007 because it's free to start a newsletter. We built a brand, talked to our users, tried to have an authentic voice.

**Alexa:** My founding insight was personal: I graduated from a great school, worked on Wall Street, was good at math and economics — and when it came to basic questions about my own wallet, I had no idea what I was doing. How many credit cards should I have? What's a credit score exactly? How much should I spend on rent? The fact that there were no clear answers for normal people was wild.

**Alexa:** We started with female Millennials as our audience, focused on that voice, and then once we raised enough money to build product, we launched a budgeting app — a single place to see all your finances. Then customers started writing in: "I really want to talk to someone." So we got licensed as a Registered Investment Advisor and opened up paid advice. We made the pricing honest: $500 a year for access to unlimited advice from certified financial planners. No hidden fees, no product commissions. You could trust the advice was actually right for you.

**Alexa:** By the time we got acquired, companies were buying LearnVest software as an employee benefit — like health insurance or 401(k). That's what LearnVest became: the largest fastest-growing online financial planning company.

## From 0 to 2.5 Million Users Without Advertising [00:24:00]

**Sam:** How long from content to product?

**Alexa:** We launched our first 10,000 users about a year after I moved to New York. We did a free 10-day Boot Camp program — ten days to learn your wallet basics — and one day we looked up and 10,000 people had signed up. From there we kept building and kept listening.

**Sam:** How big was the audience by acquisition?

**Alexa:** About 2.5 million total users. About half a million who had linked all their financial accounts. About 100,000 paying customers when we got acquired.

**Sam:** And you basically didn't spend on ads?

**Alexa:** By the year we got acquired, we'd spent less than $1 million on advertising total. We grew through business partnerships, content, relationships, and books. Very old school.

## On Zuckerberg and Facebook [00:31:00]

**Shaan:** You were at Harvard undergrad when Facebook launched at Harvard.

**Alexa:** I could never forget. I was one of the early users — somewhere in the hundreds by user number. It was immediately the most useful thing on the planet. I always joked with my husband: he asked me out before Facebook existed and I literally couldn't remember if it was him or someone else. I couldn't look it up.

**Sam:** Did you know Zuckerberg personally?

**Alexa:** I met him a handful of times undergrad. I know his family and siblings pretty well. But I actually wanted to drop out of Harvard to start LearnVest. My mom said: just finish college first. I'm glad she convinced me. But seeing Facebook get built from inside Harvard — that was the "Facebook effect." When you see what the internet can do, if you're hardworking and have skills, you think: maybe I should go build something.

## The Sale: $375M, Northwestern Mutual [00:38:00]

**Sam:** You were doing less than $10 million in revenue and you got $375 million — that's a massive multiple. Why?

**Alexa:** We were doing more revenue than that. But the bigger thing is what we built. We had about eight patents on financial planning software that didn't exist anywhere else.

**Alexa:** Almost all existing financial planning software was designed for wealthy people trying to grow more wealth. It didn't handle cash flow — "can you pay your bills?" But 78% of America is paycheck to paycheck. The software that existed would never have worked for the majority of the country.

**Alexa:** We built cash flow-based financial planning software that started from: how much cash do you have? Can you pay your bills? If there's anything left, where does it go? That was genuinely new. Northwestern Mutual had five million-plus families and a CEO who understood that plugging our software in could change how they served all of them.

**Sam:** Were you profitable?

**Alexa:** Near profitable — we could have been. We had just raised $35 million and were growing. The important thing is we were incredibly lean. We had $52 million in cash on the balance sheet when we sold. I was a recession entrepreneur. We always ran the company like we didn't have extra money.

**Sam:** Why raise money and then just sit on it?

**Alexa:** We raised it, then Northwestern Mutual invested and then said they wanted to acquire us. So the timing was what it was.

## The CEO Job at Acquisition Time [00:48:00]

**Shaan:** What was the decision like? Did you want to sell?

**Alexa:** My job as CEO was to gather all the options unemotionally and bring them to the board. I had other acquirers at the table. And staying private was genuinely a good option — we had $52 million in cash, we were growing, and the B2B business (selling LearnVest software as an employee benefit) was exploding. That was way more fun than signing up 40 households a day.

**Alexa:** I waited until the morning we signed to make my own decision, because until then my job was gathering options for the board. When I made the call, I thought: Northwestern Mutual has 8,000 financial advisors who are as passionate about this mission as I am. If they can get our software to five million families instead of 100,000 — that's actually a better use of what we built.

**Shaan:** Any regrets?

**Alexa:** None. The CEO John Schlifske was one of the best leaders I've ever worked with. A third of Northwestern Mutual's management team eventually came from the original LearnVest team. I stayed four years. Friends who'd also sold companies kept asking me: how did you stay four years? I loved it. There were brutal days. At one point we retired the LearnVest brand, which was painful — but it was the right call. It was a 163-year-old brand and LearnVest was tiny.

## The Future: Self-Driving Wallets [01:00:00]

**Shaan:** What spaces are interesting to you now?

**Alexa:** At Inspired Capital we're generalist — seed to Series A. Right now we're looking at food tech, the future of money movement, trucking and logistics. But the vision I can't stop thinking about is what I call the self-driving wallet.

**Alexa:** Here's the problem: most personal finance software today shows you charts and suggests things. You make the decisions. But most people don't want to make decisions about their money. They want the decisions made for them. What if you just set your rules — I need $400 in my checking account at all times, anything above that goes to the exact right place — and the software just did it?

**Sam:** Right, like you're not really "driving" anymore — the car changes lanes because it knows where you're trying to go.

**Alexa:** Exactly. And I can mathematically tell you where your next dollar should go to save you money and make your life better. This isn't a cure-for-cancer problem where we don't know the answer. It's math. The problem is the infrastructure.

## Why ACH Rails Break Self-Driving Money [01:10:00]

**Sam:** What's the infrastructure problem exactly?

**Alexa:** Money movement. Your bank accounts are probably in seven places right now. Moving money between them runs over ACH rails from 1971. That typically takes five days. If you want a wallet that self-drives in real time — sweeping money to where it should go every hour — you need instant money movement. And we don't have that in the US.

**Alexa:** Europe is a decade ahead. They're on real-time payments rails. You can move money instantly. We have something called RTP (Real-Time Payments) starting to come online in the US, but it's not universal.

**Sam:** And the "why do we get paid every two weeks" thing connects to this?

**Alexa:** Yes. You work every day. You should get paid every day — or by the hour, or in real-time as you earn. It's a free loan to your employer that we all just accept because that's how it's always been done. Uber already pays drivers after every ride. That's the direction everything should go. You earn it, it instantly sweeps to pay your credit card balance, fund your 401(k) match, hit your savings target. Friction everywhere eliminated.

**Sam:** What prevents this from happening faster?

**Alexa:** The ACH rail problem. And for business owners, there's a real concern: small businesses float payroll on that lag. You've got five days where the money is sitting there and you're using it to collect from clients before making payroll. That's a real function for small businesses.

## COVID, Remote Work, and 40 Million Lost Jobs [01:20:00]

**Alexa:** Can I say something about what's happening right now? [Recording is May 2020.]

**Sam:** Please.

**Alexa:** I think we started 2020 and we're essentially ending 2030 in terms of how much we've accelerated the ball. Remote work, the gig economy, money movement. Some of it is genuinely exciting. Working parents like me are getting two extra hours a day with kids that we were losing to commuting.

**Alexa:** But here's what I wake up at 3am thinking about: 40 million Americans need to get back to work, and a lot of those jobs are not coming back. Main Street and Wall Street are completely disconnected right now. The markets look fine. Tens of millions of families can't pay their bills. I'm a certified financial planner who stared at America's wallets for a decade. The headline is: people can't afford anything, and most of them are terrified to even look at their bank account. That hasn't changed. COVID just exposed how precarious the whole thing was.

## On Coming from a Normal Background [01:28:00]

**Shaan:** You're investing from New York and went to Harvard and your business partner's family is a big deal — but you're from Jacksonville, Florida. Do you feel like an insider or do you still feel connected to your roots?

**Alexa:** I'm so glad you asked. My dad was an Army doctor. My mom went to boot camp, was a nurse. My dad passed away when I was younger — I was raised by a single mom. Both my brothers are doctors. The bubble I live in now is completely ridiculous, and I need to remember that every day.

**Alexa:** I stared at America's wallets for a decade. The headline is: people can't afford anything and they're stressed. If you don't have dignity — if you can't put food on the table for your family — that's all-consuming stress. And I remember being too scared to log into my bank account. That PTSD doesn't fully go away. That's exactly why I care about building technology that removes friction from people's financial lives.
