## How He's Built a 40+ Unicorn Portfolio [00:01:30]

**Sam:** So the intro for you is basically: you're a super angel investor. You've invested in a bunch of companies — I think you have something like 40-plus unicorns in the portfolio, and impressively, at least 20 or 30 of them were very early stage. Seeds, Series A, pre-seed. That's wild. As somebody who's invested in, I don't know, 100 companies myself, I look at what you've done and that is prolific. So first question — there's a block of wood and I don't really know how to chop into this, so I'm just going to swing one blunt swing here: how the heck are you doing this? How have you had this success in angel investing?

**Elad:** I mean, it's probably two or three things. Number one, I think I was just lucky. I started investing at a good time when there was still a lot to be done in SaaS and consumer and a variety of areas — and obviously that's also true in AI now.

Second, I tend to take more of a market-first approach than most early-stage people. I focus on the market and product-market fit more than the founders. Obviously founders are incredibly important — I started two companies myself, so I hope they're incredibly important — but I also think what you're actually building and which market you're in for who matters a lot. I've seen great people crushed by terrible markets, and I've seen reasonably mediocre people do really well.

And then lastly, I really try to follow the technology and the changes that are happening in technology. I got involved with a lot of generative AI companies quite early — Perplexity, Harvey, things like that — simply because I was really keen on generative AI and ML-related stuff. I started investing in all those things three years ago, before ChatGPT was out, before Midjourney, before these things became a trend. Sometimes you end up early in some really important areas simply because you're technology-first or market-change-first.

**Sam:** Were you just doing your own money at first, or did you have a fund from the get-go?

**Elad:** Initially I was just investing my own money, and then I ran out pretty fast. I hadn't made that much money at Google and I invested almost all of it — like over 50% of it.

**Sam:** What does that mean, "running out"? You initially had wealth from selling your business, and then you just put all of it into private companies?

**Elad:** I put a huge amount of my own money into private companies. Yeah. One of my big regrets is somebody pinged me on a SpaceX round when it was like a $500 million company. I was really excited about it — I thought it was such an interesting bet — but I just didn't have enough money for the minimum check size.

**Shaan:** Where did you go once you deployed your own capital?

**Elad:** I started effectively raising small SPVs, and then that morphed into funds. Now I have kind of a tiramisu cake of different structures — different layers I can deploy from, depending on the deal. Personal investments, fund investments, very large things would be fund plus SPV. For example, I led or co-led the last two rounds for Applied Intuition. I've led rounds for all sorts of companies at this point.

**Shaan:** Are there any other partners at your shop, or are you the guy?

**Elad:** I've recently hired a few people to help me in different ways. But fundamentally what I want to do is continue to build things, work on things, think about technology — what I'd almost call the anti-VC approach. Be builder-first.

There are a lot of side projects my team and I are now working on. For example, we're trying to take the thousand most important books that are off copyright and translate them into dozens of languages using AI, then create audiobooks from all of them. So people can download the great works of history from anywhere in the world in any language. We're doing stuff like that — and it also forces us to really use different AI tools, compare ElevenLabs versus Suno versus OpenAI, figure out how you actually translate a long-form book with high consistency, how you QA that.

**Sam:** Are you doing that because it could be a business, or because you're thinking "this is an interesting project and doing it will teach me interesting things"?

**Elad:** I'm doing it because I think it's cool and societally useful. There are multiple aspects of why to work on something, and this one is a little bit less short-term and more like: this seems useful and interesting.

**Sam:** Were you always that way? Because I only recently started feeling like "I want to do something because it's cool" — I guess now that I'm financially secure. **Elad:** I've always been that way. I did degrees in math and biology and a PhD in biology because I thought it was societally useful — you're not going to make money as a PhD in biology unless something magical happens. My driver has always been what is useful societally.

The other thing I'd say about it: it forces you to go deeper than people tend to go on things they're just kind of casually interested in. You go to that next extra level of depth, using things hands-on. I think that also matters in terms of gaining real intuition for certain things.

## Market-First Investing: What It Actually Means [00:12:00]

**Shaan:** Most businesses have one or two things that are the fundamental aspect of that business that matters, and everything else is noise. Where do people go wrong — caring about five things when there's actually only one that matters?

**Elad:** Well, with Stripe, their one insight was: how do we get every developer using this product? We're not going to worry about mid-market and enterprise. How do we get every early-stage technology company that matters onto us?

**Shaan:** That sounds great in hindsight. But I own a business now, and it's really hard to come up with that insight.

**Elad:** It's hard. But that's usually the key question. And different types of businesses may have that characteristic or not — some complex businesses may have more. I invested in Anduril, and there were about five things I thought a next-gen defense tech company had to do to become a "prime" — one of the really big companies in the market. Without all five, you're just not going to make it. But I think 90% of the time it boils down to one core belief, or maybe one thing you just need to see happen in the business to believe everything else will fall into place.

## How He Learns a New Market: The Anduril Story [00:18:00]

**Sam:** You said you invest in markets. When Anduril was first started, most people had no idea about that industry. When you're trying to master a new topic, what is your process?

**Elad:** With Anduril, the main reason I got interested in defense tech was because the big tech companies were running away from it. Google shut down Maven and I was like, of course we're going to need national defense — whether you're a Democrat or Republican, you want to protect Western values, there's conflict in the world, there are bad actors.

**Sam:** To push back: I'd say, yeah, but there are 10 other companies in that space. They've got it covered. How do you learn enough to know there's actually an interesting opportunity?

**Elad:** A few things. Obviously you just read what you can. You look at market structure and growth rates. You look at technology shifts. And then you go and do the work — you look at what the companies are doing, whether the market is consolidating or not, what the growth rate is, what the margin structure looks like.

For example, traditional defense tech companies operate on cost-plus contracts — if you have a million-dollar drone, they charge 5% on top. They can't make more than 50K margin. Anduril can sell you a much better drone for a million dollars and make 50% margin, which is traditional hardware margin — 10x the margin dollars. One of the things people don't appreciate about Anduril as a business is that it represents a market cap expansion in the defense world because you have a higher-margin, higher-leverage business model.

**Sam:** Did you hear Palmer talking and then go research the market, or had you independently been curious about defense?

**Elad:** It goes back to what I was saying: Google shut down Maven and I thought, what a wonderful opportunity for a startup. So I actually called someone I knew at Google who was working on Google Cloud — the Maven team was part of that — and I said, "Did all the people who wanted to do Maven quit in protest?" And he said, "No, no, it was all the people who were against Maven who quit. The rest are still here." So I was like, okay — where do I find somebody who wants to work on this? I started poking around, and I ran into Trae at something, and we started talking, and he mentioned he was working on it. I got super excited and invested in their first round — or they were kind enough to let me, I should say.

**Shaan:** That's way more impressive than just meeting Palmer at a party. The fact that you arrived at that conclusion independently — that's pretty remarkable.

**Elad:** I think I was fortunate. The best people I get to interact with have this same laid-back approach: "I just kind of do the work, I was lucky," and then you start peeling back layers and realize, oh, this thing you called luck was actually you. I read that Maven story too. I didn't think to myself, "What a wonderful opportunity." I just moved on with my life.

**Shaan:** Right. That's the thing about luck — number two or three on the "four levels of luck" framework is that luck favors the prepared mind. Because you knew what you were looking for, you could spot the opportunity when it presented itself. The average person might see that same story and not understand what they're looking at.

## On Running a Lean Investment Operation [00:27:00]

**Sam:** I think according to articles about you, you had like two employees and had raised something like a billion dollars. Sam Altman has talked about how a one- or two-person billion-dollar company is going to be a thing because of AI. But that's kind of what you've already done.

**Elad:** There's a long history of this. If you look at the world's biggest hedge funds or a lot of later-stage investment firms, they tend to have that structure. There's usually one or two people who do most of the good investments. Minecraft is a canonical example — it had a very small team when Microsoft acquired it.

But there's also a need to go big sometimes. Sometimes you want to win the market and you should hire a ton of people. I think the future of tiny AI companies is very overstated in the medium term. AI is largely going to be business as usual, and certain types of teams are going to shrink a lot. I'm an investor in a company called Decagon, for example, that's working on the customer success side — that's an area where I think there'll be enormous leverage to existing CS reps, but probably an overall shrinkage in that industry overall. Certain teams will get impacted first.

## Missionary, Mercenary, and Artist [00:32:00]

**Sam:** You don't seem Money-motivated. That's a really challenging person to compete against, because that's often the type who wins. Missionary versus mercenary — you know what I mean?

**Elad:** I know what you mean. I think Naval has a great framework on this. John Doerr used to ask founders in the 90s, "Are you a missionary or a mercenary?" — and of course you had to say "missionary," right? How could you say "mercenary"?

Naval's framework is: well, of course early in your career you're at least partially a mercenary, otherwise you're never going to find the opportunity and go in on it. Then later in life you're a missionary — you're not zero-sum, you're trying to do things for the greater good. And then once you've made it in your career, you should become an artist. You should do it for the love of the craft.

For me, the motivator is: technology is a lever on the world. That's why I was trying to do biology. That's why I went into tech. How do you do the important things in the world? If you're working on the most important problems with the best people, you probably end up with a good financial return because you're in the middle of the stuff that matters. But that doesn't have to be the motivating goal.

I'll give you an example: I was once sitting at a friend's wedding dinner, seated at a table with a bunch of investors from a VC firm. I said to the guy next to me, "Hey, what are you interested in these days?" He said, "Crypto." I said, "Oh great — we can talk about distributed consensus and trustless systems and censorship-resistant products." I said, "What are you excited about in crypto?" He looks me in the eyes and says, "You can make a sh*t load of money at it." I started laughing — I thought he was joking. He was not.

## Big Ideas: A New K–12 School Chain [00:37:00]

**Sam:** On your website you publish ideas you're interested in or want people to help you pull off. One that caught my eye: you said you want to do a new chain of K–12 schools inspired by ancient Greece. What's that about?

**Elad:** I think fundamentally anybody who has kids realizes there are certain aspects of the education system that are overemphasized or underemphasized. It feels like there are four or five things that are really useful for kids to learn, and I think it would be great to help establish something that could be used broadly — across cities or regions — that really focuses on some of those basic aspects of learning.

**Sam:** What did the ancient Greeks do that you're trying to emulate?

**Elad:** It's really just used as an example — you need some framework with consistency and a compass. There was a certain emphasis on reasoning, logic, thinking, discourse, and debate. There was an emphasis on mathematics, on writing, on understanding. That's when Archimedes emerged. That's when early interesting mathematics emerged. That's when great philosophical treatises were written. There was also a lot of emphasis on beauty and art, on being physically fit and resilient.

There's a school in San Francisco called Proof School that I find fascinating. Their line is basically "for kids who love math," and they have a very strong emphasis on mathematics throughout the curriculum. Kids are doing machine learning in 10th or 11th grade. I find that kind of inspiring — they said, "We're going to take a different lens on this, and our lens is just kids who love math. Let's figure out the right curriculum for those kids."

**Sam:** What's your plan with this school thing — are you looking for an entrepreneur to spearhead it?

**Elad:** Yeah, I need somebody to spearhead it. It's really easy to come up with ideas — then usually you need help executing them. So it's something I'd be happy to sponsor, but I'd really need to find somebody who can drive that day-to-day. My kids are very happy in their current schools, so it's more just something I think would be societally useful to do more broadly. And especially if you can do it as a chain, you end up with some consistency and the ability to bring it to different populations that may not otherwise have access to this kind of schooling.

## Big Ideas: Longevity and the Bioage Story [00:44:00]

**Shaan:** You mentioned a company called BioAge and a new drug for muscle. You have my attention. What is this drug, and what's exciting to you about that space?

**Elad:** BioAge just went public and is in their quiet period, so there's probably not a ton I should say about them specifically. I was an early investor — I think I was the first investor in that company actually. The CEO is an exceptional PhD and postdoc who I believe did her postdoc at Stanford in biogerontology. They now have different drugs in different phases of clinical trials. That should all be in their S-1 and public financial materials, which I'd encourage people to look at.

But in general, I think aging is an area that's been dramatically underinvested in. Part of that is because of the structure of big pharma — that industry tends to have very old companies driving the agenda, with all the implications that brings. It's also highly regulated. And if you look at the National Institute on Aging, it has a very small budget relative to the NCI for cancer or other parts of the NIH, and most of that budget just goes to Alzheimer's. Actual fundamental research into aging is quite sparse.

We know that aging is a developmental program that can be perturbed. I actually worked on that for my PhD — I worked at the intersection of aging, longevity, cancer, and insulin signaling. There are evolutionarily conserved pathways that, if you tweak them, you end up with organisms that live a lot longer. You can knock out a gene in C. elegans and it lives two or three times longer as a healthy adult. We know there are drugs that do that in mice.

**Sam:** Can you explain what you mean by "developmental program"?

**Elad:** Sure. There's one theory of aging where you just accumulate damage over time and systems break — mechanical damage from sunlight, UV, whatever. And then there's another view of aging as almost like a developmental program regulated by signals in your body — just as you go from baby to child to adult, that's a developmental program. Different organisms have different clocks for aging. Why can turtles live so long? Why do certain animals die so young? It's like a genetically defined set lifespan. Why would we have an off button? There are theories, but I don't know which is right.

What we do know is that there are certain drugs that extend lifespan in multiple organisms. Rapamycin is the classic example in mice — mice can live 10 to 30% longer on it, with the effect varying by sex. And if you can make things live much longer using drugs, and we're doing very little of that in humans, the question is why. The answer is basically three things: it's not funded enough, from a regulatory perspective the FDA hasn't had a good definition of what it means to develop an aging drug until very recently, and the broader medical world doesn't consider aging a disease — it's considered a natural state, so why develop a drug against it?

**Shaan:** Is there a company called Loyal that does aging in dogs? I think Kevin Rose talked to us about it.

**Elad:** I think so. Going after dogs first both has an easier regulatory pathway and is good for society. My dog living longer — I support nothing more than that.

**Sam:** Are you taking any interesting pharmaceuticals right now? Metformin? Anything you think will be more mainstream in three to five years?

**Elad:** The honest answer is I don't know. I've tried to stay away from taking anything chronically unless I think it would have a huge impact, and so far there isn't anything in the market that maps to that for me. A lot of it honestly is: exercise, sleep, eat well. There's reasonably good data around rapamycin, and people should work with their doctor on that. But I haven't done anything there. I've tried to wait until there's something I feel has a big enough impact.

## Big Ideas: Monumental — Public Art and the Case for Inspiring Infrastructure [00:58:00]

**Shaan:** What's the Monuments idea? I think that one is dope.

**Elad:** So one thing I need somebody to come work with me on — and I'd happily fund it — is this: if you look at every society at its peak, they would build large-scale monuments towards progress. The Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. The Eiffel Tower — which was built in the late 1800s for a World's Fair in Paris to show off French steelmaking prowess. It was an ode to technology, and it was very controversial at the time. People thought it was ugly.

A lot of bridges were built in America in that same era. The Eads Bridge in St. Louis — they would do these incredible construction projects and then do funny things like have an elephant walk across to prove it's strong. There's a flex side of it and an inspirational side: where are the large-scale inspiring pieces of beauty and art in society anymore? Where have they gone? Why aren't we doing those?

**Sam:** The Vegas Sphere — is that not the last one?

**Elad:** No, seriously — the Sphere is a real example of large-scale societal beauty. And you know what another example is? The Blue Angels. I was having a debate with someone about how much money the American government spends on air shows, and I was like, that is totally worth it. Whenever you go to one, you feel a real sense of patriotism and awe. Same with going to the Moon in the 60s and 70s — we spent a significant amount of money on that, arguably more than we've spent on longevity research, which is actually a shame. But there is a history in America of doing things just to flex, just to inspire. What's the last thing we did like that? It's been decades.

**Shaan:** There was a period from around 1870 — post-Civil War — up to the 1960s when JFK was assassinated, where America did a whole series of interesting experiments that you could just say, "We're doing it because we can."

**Elad:** Part of the shift is regulation, and part of it is this odd post-modern cynical thinking — a very strong anti-progress movement. The basic idea of Monumental — which is what we're calling the project — is: you buy land, you construct a large monument that is inspiring. Imagine if you walked out in San Francisco, looked across to the view with the Golden Gate Bridge and Alcatraz, and there was a giant statue that was an ode to the future. How much do these things even cost?

Actually, there was an island for sale in the bay recently. I looked at it a couple of times — I thought, maybe this is the thing, maybe this is an anchor. But it's going to be so hard to do anything in the Bay Area. They can't even construct a public toilet. Are they really going to let you build a beautiful monument on any reasonable time frame?

There are lots of other metro areas that are much more amenable. The land in major metros is usually the expensive part. The construction isn't that bad — really what you're building is a large statue. In some cases you're building a base or foundation that may be part of a building, maybe an elevator if you have a good view, but you're not building a large commercial property with all the plumbing and electrical. These are kind of like hollow shells.

**Sam:** What would be our equivalent of the Statue of Liberty or the Eiffel Tower — something that demonstrates something meaningful about our moment?

**Elad:** The intention around Monumental is partly to solicit ideas from local people — what do they want, what do they view as inspiring, what would drive their children's optimism forward? It's an ode to progress, but more importantly, it's a way to encourage inspiration in young people. How do you get people jazzed about the future, about technology, about optimism, about building things and moving society forward? I think part of this lack of ongoing great works is a reflection of the cynicism that came in during the 1960s and 70s.

**Sam:** That's one of my favorite eras to read about. In the mid-60s — Malcolm X, JFK, RFK — there's this series of assassinations, and one can argue that's the death of innocence in America.

**Shaan:** Well, you know what? I think it only takes one. Elon showed that when he started doing projects that no other entrepreneur really wanted to do and no investors really wanted to fund, and it created a wave. Tesla and SpaceX are our moon mission. This guy poured his entire talent and net worth into super high-risk, low-probability, useful-for-society, hard-but-awesome projects — and he pulled it off. That changed the wiring in every founder's brain. Doesn't even matter what you're working on — that example still lives in your brain.

## Life Advice: Intensive Travel and Community [01:18:00]

**Sam:** You're a pretty serious person. You see problems and you want to solve them. What do you do just for fun? Just to unwind?

**Elad:** Before you have kids there are all sorts of things that disappear once you do. I used to do what I'd call intensive travel. I'd go to India for two months and do yoga for two months with a guy who had learned from a guy in a cave — just very immersive forms of travel.

**Sam:** If somebody today had the freedom to do that, what would be your guide to intensive travel? What was transformative for you?

**Elad:** Basically: spend at least a month in one city, and decide to learn something there. Maybe you want to go learn how to draw. Maybe you want to go to France and learn to make pastries. In my case, it was going to India and doing this very intensive yoga experience. Especially if it's part of a community — you get immersed not just in the topic but in the people around it, the local people. You're really forced to be part of something.

I know someone who was about to start a company, and I convinced him to first go do this thing because he had an extra month or two. He went down to Brazil, enrolled in some classes, and just spent a month doing it. He said it was the best month he'd spent. How often in your life can you actually get a chance to do something like that?

It's also a very different kind of travel from, say, a tour group hitting as many cities as possible in as short a time as possible. "There's the Leaning Tower of Pisa, check, we saw it, now run to the next thing." That's very different from just sitting in one place for a month or two.

**Shaan:** Yeah, I've had great experiences doing that. We used to go to Buenos Aires for four to five weeks. Live in one city, live like a local. Everywhere I'd go, I'd do two things: I'd play basketball, because that was my native fluency — even if I couldn't speak the language, I could always make my way in. And when I travel with my brother-in-law, he loves jiu-jitsu. I'll never go to a jiu-jitsu class here in the States, but we went to jiu-jitsu gyms in Spain and you immediately feel like you're one of the people. You're learning from them, they have different techniques, you don't even speak the language — but it's an incredible fast way to bond. The jiu-jitsu community is one of the most vibrant ones right now.

**Elad:** The yoga practice I used to do was exactly the same thing. You just show up, you know how it works, everybody has the same vibe, you're instantly adopted. My sense is that the jiu-jitsu community is a great example of that today — you show up anywhere in the world and you're instantly part of it, they pull you in, everybody has a common thing to talk about, a common interest, in a really positive way, even though it's a very diverse group. And there aren't that many communities like that.

There are interesting questions about how these communities form and why they persist. But this is back to my point on doing a "hobby" — it's actually plugging into a community that I think is important.
