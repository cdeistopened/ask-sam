## The Core Problem: Your Business Is a Mirror of You [00:00:00]

**Shaan:** Today we're talking about the brutal truth about startups: your business's success is completely dependent on your growth as a CEO. We're talking about what you need to be doing from zero to one to three million in revenue, and then how to scale up from there to tens of millions, even $100 million.

**Sam:** I was journaling this morning. Can I tell you what I was writing about?

**Shaan:** Yeah, but you have to start with "dear diary."

**Sam:** I was realizing that my company's growth is limited by my personal growth. My company can only grow as fast as I can grow as a leader. Have you ever experienced that?

**Shaan:** I have a similar belief — the bottleneck of any business is the psychology of the founder. It's not necessarily my growth, but my psychology is the limiting factor. Your business is basically a mirror of your strengths and weaknesses. And a lot of people don't realize it until recently — probably once you're out of the brute force stage, somewhere around $3 million in revenue, maybe $1 million a year in profit. Before that you can just brute-force your way. After that, the hard part is: how do you scale as a leader, because your job becomes leading people, not actually doing.

## Abdication vs. Delegation [00:03:00]

**Sam:** The first shift — and the one I'm having to break — is abdicating versus delegating. Tell me if you've done this exact same thing.

I hire someone, sit down with them, and say: "So, here's what I do. I do this, this, this, and this. All right, great. There you go. Talk to me later and let me know how you've made this better." Have you done that?

**Shaan:** Yeah. You got this, right? Good. Your problem, not mine. I'm mentally relieved of duties.

**Sam:** And then the next week: "Why aren't the numbers going up? It's been a month. I showed you how to do this." I've had that conversation so many times in my life.

The difference is abdication versus proper delegation. So what is proper delegation?

I actually have it written down. The framework is: what, how, when, and motivation. You explain what you expect. You show them how to do it by training. You give very specific deadlines. You have scheduled follow-ups. And sometimes it takes months to properly train people.

For example with salespeople: you watch their sales calls and sit down for two hours every single week and say, "Instead of saying this, try this next time. Let's see what happens." Or: "You said this. Often that doesn't get the result you want because of X, Y, Z. Do this instead, here's a script." After three or four months of that, they start learning to be better than you. They'll say, "By the way, you didn't think about this — I actually went this way and got a better result." That's when you know it's working.

## The RACI Model for Accountability [00:08:00]

**Shaan:** Have you ever used the RACI model?

**Sam:** What's that?

**Shaan:** R-A-C-I. One thing that happens in a company is it becomes unclear who's got it.

Imagine we're playing catch. I throw the ball. You're the only one there. You better catch it. No doubt that you need to go after it. Now, add a second person. I throw the same ball but didn't say who it's for. I don't know if you're getting it, I don't know if we're supposed to fight over it, I don't know what to do. Now add a third, a fourth. By the time you get to seven people, some people fight for it with sharp elbows, some people are frustrated others aren't helping, some people feel resentful they never get the ball. The ball hits the floor more often when there's seven people than when there was one — which is a common dynamic in companies.

So here's how you solve it. When you define a project, you don't just set the goal and deadline. You define the RACI model.

**R** is who is *responsible* for doing it — the person driving the action forward. **A** is who is *accountable* — usually that person's boss. So let's say you're the chief revenue officer and under you is the head of sales. The head of sales is responsible for implementing the plan and getting the close rate from 15% to 19%. But the buck stops with the CRO — they're accountable for the result, not responsible for doing the work.

**C** is who gets *consulted*. Who else do I need to talk to? Maybe I need to loop in the head of delivery because I'm closing a ton of sales but overloading the delivery team, causing churn. I didn't consult him before launching this promotion. So the consulted party gets input rights — they don't drive it, but they need to weigh in.

**I** is who gets *informed* — who needs an FYI, who needs to be in the loop, but doesn't need to take action or give input.

You define that at the beginning, and that's how you prevent the "seven people, ball on the floor" problem.

**Sam:** A lot of people listening want to start a company. For the first $3 million in revenue, you can kind of ignore this and brute-force your way through. But this transition will happen faster than you think. And what separates the winners from the losers is embracing this stuff. It doesn't feel natural — it actually feels incredibly unnatural for people who start stuff. But what separates the pretty decent from the great is actually following these rules.

## Hard Conversations and Feedback [00:15:00]

**Shaan:** Something like if you're in a meeting and the person is on their phone — there's actually a proper way to correct that.

**Sam:** Snatch it?

**Shaan:** What I prefer over Nonviolent Communication is a book called The Motive. The basic premise: to be a CEO or leader, a large percentage of your time is going to be having difficult conversations. And the best way to have them is:

A — you get to it early. If you see someone isn't paying attention in a meeting, you don't wait for it to fix itself. You correct small things fast, because that will have a lot of leverage later on. They'll learn early on based on small things what you expect on the big things.

B — when you do have the conversation, you do it privately. And you frame it like: "Look, I know that you don't mean to be this way, but here's how you come off, and I'd like it if instead you did this. Does that sound good?"

The temptation is to think: "Who am I to tell them not to look at their phone? It was probably a one-time mistake, they'll figure it out." The Motive says no — correct it early.

**Sam:** Have you ever read The One Minute Manager?

**Shaan:** Years ago. What was the takeaway?

**Sam:** It's a very thin book — you can read it in 25 minutes. Written like a fable. The core of it is two things I changed from doing.

First is planning. I used to either be top-down or bottoms-up. Top-down: I tell you exactly what we're going to do and you execute. Then I'm disappointed you didn't execute it the way it existed in my head. Bottom-up: you come up with the plans, I criticize them and try to improve them.

The one-minute plan is a third way. The employee writes what they think they should focus on and what they're going to do. The manager gives feedback. The employee revises. You iterate until you arrive at a plan they wrote that you agree with. You get the best of both worlds — they have ownership, they did the thinking, but you're totally on board with the result.

The second thing is around feedback. I learned the acronym PICS and NICS. Positive feedback should be positive, immediate, and certain. Negative feedback should be negative, immediate, and certain.

How do people get feedback wrong? The feedback sandwich — positive, negative, positive. The person leaves confused. Or they delay it. Something's negative, they let it slide. Next time they let it slide. Fourth time, they've seen four of these, they come at you with an avalanche, and you're like: "Where did that come from?" And they're like: "You've had so many chances, how dare you?"

Nobody wins. So do it immediately. Right after the meeting. "Hey, I really liked the way you did that." Or: "I noticed that, and that felt like an elevated version of what you were doing before. Keep it up." Make it certain — say specifically what behavior, what effect it had, what you want more or less of. Not wishy-washy.

## Company Values — Name Them Late [00:24:00]

**Shaan:** I've taken a different stance on company values than I had before. When I started my last company, I wrote the values out in advance and failed to live up to them. When I started Hampton, I said: I'm actually not going to write down values. I'm going to start this company, see how it feels, and name the values later.

**Sam:** Jack Smith baby naming.

**Shaan:** Yes! My friend Jack had a baby and refused to name her for the first year. I said: "Jack, that's absolutely insane." He said: "I think I should get to know her first before we give her a name." And I started thinking about it — and realized that's actually brilliant.

I did the same thing with my company. What bothers me? What attributes do I particularly love when I see us do here? And after a couple years, we're now naming our values — and it feels like 100% the better move. Before, when I named values early, it felt like a prison. I made them when I was 26. By 28, I didn't even align with them anymore. But I'm stuck with this.

So what are the three? The first is asked someone who's the first person to ever ask me about my company values.

**Sam:** What are they?

**Shaan:** First: **fun**. I value fun. That means in our copy I want to do silly stuff just because it's exciting. I don't want to follow data all the time. Sometimes we're going to do something just because it's fun. And I'm going to hire people I have fun being around.

Second: **speed**. I value moving fast more than I previously thought.

Third, which is a bit controversial: **pride**. What does pride mean? We started off virtually — the data showed we could sell it and make money virtually. But it didn't feel good. I didn't take pride in that. I took pride in saying we have real in-person events, even though the data didn't exactly support it at the time. Or if we're going to lay people off, I'm going to go above and beyond and give them more severance than market rate. When a vendor submits a bill, even though we have net 90, I'll pay them that day. It feels good to do the right thing and have a reputation for it.

**Shaan:** Someone showed me a reminder text we were sending to potential customers. And I was like: "Show some pride. That's a boring boilerplate message." And that actually changed behavior. I told someone to show some pride, and in this small thing they did.

**Sam:** The problem with just saying "speed" as a value is everyone agrees with it in the abstract. When you ask who agrees with moving fast, everyone raises their hand. When nobody says they want to move slow — that's when you know the value isn't real yet.

**Shaan:** Zuckerberg said something about this when he was a young CEO: you need to acknowledge the trade-off in the value itself. Instead of "speed," he said "move fast and break things." He explicitly acknowledged: when you move really fast, things are going to break. By putting it in the value, he made it real. He made it something not every company would agree with. It made it something you could be criticized on.

I'm still wordsmithing. But I don't think most people are comfortable when you say move fast. Someone will say this is going to take a quarter, and I'll ask: "What would it take to see results in two weeks?" That actually rubs a huge percentage of people the wrong way. The people who kick ass at my company are the ones who say: "Huh, interesting question. If we do this, this, and this — we could actually pull that off."

**Sam:** I think you should make a public list — wall of love style — of: here's the value, and here's a running list of concrete anecdotes where that's actually true. See how long you can get the list, and who's adding to it. Because when you write it down, you realize: we say that, but what do we actually do that's fast? That's when it becomes real.

## WWE Belt and Weekly Reinforcement [00:34:00]

**Sam:** I used to do something that is not what they teach at Harvard Business School. I bought a replica WWE Championship belt. Every Friday I would call the team over — everybody away from the computer, grab a beer or a drink, stand in a little huddle. 12-15 people at the company. And I would basically give the belt to somebody.

The belt holder got small perks — dessert first at lunch, that kind of thing. And whoever had the belt, I got to tell the story of what they did that was in line with our values. Not the most senior person. The person who did something above and beyond, out of the ordinary, in line with one of the values. I'd tell the value story, tell what they did, and hand them the belt.

You do that 52 weeks in a row and people start to get it. No different than second-grade class — people learn what gets rewarded around here.

I also assigned a prize. $1,000 in cash. The rule was you could only spend it on something you could tell us a story about the following week. Not "I paid my rent." It's got to be story-worthy. So that person got to stand up with pride, come home and tell their girlfriend or wife: "I won the belt, I got a grand, I did this thing." You productize the value and reinforce it weekly.

**Shaan:** Alex Hormozi says something great: most of your team doesn't need to be taught — they need to be reminded. You teach something once, and then you just constantly remind. It's almost always a lack of reminding, not a lack of teaching.

## Edge Founders: Speed-Running the Same Business [00:39:00]

**Shaan:** I want to talk about a type of founder that I find fascinating — what I call "edge founders who go all-in the second time on their edge talents."

The founder of RX Bar, Peter. He sold RX Bar for around $600 million. A few years later, we're like: "Peter, what are you excited about? What's the next move?" He says: "I'm launching a protein bar." He went back to the exact same market with a new product — David Bar — and it's already over $100 million in revenue in less than two years post-launch.

It's like somebody speedrunning a video game. They know the level so well they can do it with a level of mastery a newcomer can't possibly have.

I'm the opposite. If I have a success, the last thing I want to do is go do it again. But there's this fascinating group of people that do.

**Sam:** I do the same playbook, actually. I looked at it this morning — everything I've done for 15 years has been content and community. When I moved to San Francisco, I had a book club. That turned into blogging, a newsletter. That turned into HustleCon, which had 10,000 people in 2018. Then Trends. Then Hampton. It's content as a magnet to attract a community, and then monetizing the community. That's been my one thing the whole time.

**Shaan:** Even more specifically: you see a community you want to be part of, use content as a magnet to bring people in, and the business is the community. HustleCon was charge-once. Trends was charge annually. Hampton is charge annually at a higher price. You've just been doing the same playbook smarter and better each time.

## The Carbone Pasta Sauce Story [00:44:00]

**Shaan:** The CEO of Rao's sauce — you know this story?

**Sam:** Rao's, that one Manhattan Italian restaurant. And now this massive brand.

**Shaan:** Rao's sold and scaled up, eventually hitting a $2 billion valuation. The CEO who ran that company then goes and does the exact same thing with Carbone. Different restaurant, same playbook.

He spent four months in the lab getting the flavors right. Then tried to get into retail — knocked on every door, no interest. Couldn't get his foot in the door for about 18 months.

So he had to think about position. His Don Draper hat: this is restaurant-quality sauce, we use San Marzano tomatoes, cooked on an open kettle over fire. We're going to price this at twice what competitors charge. At $7 to $11, that's expensive. But what that means is that a retailer who normally makes 50 cents on a sauce now makes $2.50. They have a big incentive to carry it.

And that pitch started to work. Now they're north of $100 million in sales, in 27,000 stores.

The same guy, running the pasta playbook again.

## The Conference Empire [00:49:00]

**Shaan:** There's a guy named Jonathan Weiner who's done the same thing with industry conferences. Go look at his LinkedIn. He started a conference in 1996 and has been doing the same thing since.

He did Money 2020 — a payments conference. Then Shoptalk — e-commerce. Then Groceryshop — food. Now Human X for AI, and Health for health innovation. His fifth or sixth conference. The previous four collectively sold for something like $600 million. Each one succeeded and became a nine-figure business again and again.

He had this playbook: $2,000 ticket, but if you're a vendor you're obligated to attend meetings with different people. He just kept running the same blueprint in different niches.

And there's a sports betting guy — I'll tell this one from the milk road podcast. He built the most popular sports betting blog in New Jersey when it was the only legal state. All his competitors said: US market's dead, we've got to go international. He said: I can just dominate New Jersey. Made a site, owned all the New Jersey sports betting traffic, sold it to FanDuel-type company for around $40 million.

Did the exact same thing in Minnesota when it opened up. Sold to the same company for $45 million. Faster this time. In the contract they were like: "Any other state, you can't do this again. We didn't put that non-compete in the first one." Then I caught up with him recently. Guess what he's doing?

**Sam:** He did it again.

**Shaan:** Running the same playbook. I could not believe it. Actually, I totally could.

## Why This Is So Hard Psychologically [00:54:00]

**Shaan:** The problem with this model is psychology. Two things happen when you know too much.

First: you have the most scar tissue. You met all the right people, you learned all the hard lessons, you know the nuances — but you also have trauma. Some people literally can't get past it emotionally. They don't want to go back into that space.

Second: if you've succeeded and your money problems are solved, you're playing for the sport of business now. And some people need novelty. They need something new. I've always been in the "need something new" category without really questioning it.

But I've met a handful of people who just run the same play and completely dominate. And I find it fascinating. The problem of course is they risk being bored of it. You're kind of sitting on a balanced seesaw — you want it to go up and down, that's kind of the point.

**Sam:** I mean, I always thought I was the kind of guy who needs novelty. But actually I think I've been doing the same thing for 16 years — just content and community, but smarter. The goal has always been: can I get this content-and-community thing to $100 million in revenue? I've never done it. I sold The Hustle too early. Austin Rief actually did it. But I'm trying again with Hampton.

**Shaan:** That's the pod. Hope that stuff helps people run their companies better.

**Sam:** All right, talk soon.
