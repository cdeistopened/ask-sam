## The Origin Story: The Day After You Sell [00:02:30]

**Sam:** What was the original idea?

**Michael:** It's really a simple idea. If you're an incredibly successful entrepreneur — could be building a business for 10, 20, 30 years — and then you sell the business, you think it's the magic moment when you sell. But actually what happens is the day after you sell, you have a lot of money but you might be alone. You might have an assistant, but you don't have a thousand employees. You don't have anybody laughing at your jokes anymore. You might even have to get your own coffee.

Everybody around you thinks you're wealthy and successful, but you've lost the platform that allowed you to feel successful. All of a sudden you're back at a new point, and the number one challenge you have is to be a wealth preserver — except you don't know anything about preserving wealth. What you are is a great entrepreneur, a great leader, a great manager. You're good at inspiring troops. But you don't have any troops anymore.

So you have this dramatic shift, and it turns out that what it takes to be a great entrepreneur might qualify you to be a mediocre investor. When you're an entrepreneur, you focus on a single opportunity. You're highly emotional about it. You give it everything you can. When you're an investor, you have to be more dispassionate. You have to have a diversified portfolio. You have to be unemotional about it.

The sum of it all is that there's a little-understood transition which a few very lucky, very successful people get to go through. We wanted to study that — which is what we've done over the last 21 years — to help people in that transition get through it and on to a better place.

**Sam:** When you originally started the business, what was the product? Was it like YPO where it was a monthly group meetup?

**Michael:** You mentioned YPO, which is a fantastic organization. There's another similar organization called Vistage, and they're both for CEOs. To give you an idea: the last time I looked, both Vistage and YPO each have about 50,000 members globally — might be 60,000 now. Vistage has 50,000 and YPO has about 30,000 each, I think.

**Sam:** These are huge businesses. Vistage charges like $50,000 a year, right?

**Michael:** I think Vistage is around $15,000 or $18,000 — I don't have the exact number, could be $20,000. YPO doesn't have a single price because depending on whether you go on the trips, people pay different amounts.

The big difference between those two — which are great organizations — is that YPO is self-facilitated by its members, whereas Vistage and TIGER 21, our groups are led by professionals. We have over 100 groups around the globe now and a cadre of over 60 professional chairs that we've trained to lead these groups.

But the bottom line is those are the two great organizations for CEOs and business owners. When you decide you want to get off the merry-go-round for whatever reason, the next decision point is best focused on with peers that you'll find in TIGER 21.

**Sam:** So basically the original premise — and you can tell me if that's still the premise — is you have a small group of peers, 12 to 15 people, and you meet on some cadence, like monthly full-day, and you have a chair who leads these discussions?

## The Psychology of Wealth: How Much Is Enough? [00:14:00]

**Sam:** You work with a significant number of wealthy people and have interesting insights. What are the levels of wealth where someone sees real changes in their life?

**Michael:** It's really a state of mind. You have people who are retired getting X dollars, and others getting 10X dollars, and yet the one receiving the lower amount might be more emotionally balanced because of meditation or inner work they've done.

The most interesting thing: there are endless studies showing that whether you ask people who earn $70,000, $700,000, or $7 million a year, "How much more do you have to earn to be happy?" — the answer is something like 20% more no matter where you are. The average person just needs just a little more to be happy. Which tells you about the unfilled nature of human ambition.

**Sam:** The way I remembered it — and I could be wrong — it was like 2x. Like if your net worth is a million, you'd feel a lot better if it were two. But they talked to people worth a hundred million and even they were like, "Yeah, but if I had 200 I think I would finally feel more comfortable."

**Michael:** Wherever you are, you have the illusion that if you had a little more it would make your life a little easier. But sometimes if you have a little more, it's a burden — you have to manage it. It's intellectually like a job.

It's just in the nature of things that the grass always seems greener on the other side. And there's another really important part: it doesn't matter whether we're talking about $3 million or $30 million. If you take two people worth $30 million — one had been worth $50 million and lost $20 million to get there, and another had been worth $20 million and made $10 million — those two people are worth the same $30 million. The person who got there by losing feels absolutely devastated, and the person who got there by gaining feels generally quite proud. Same $30 million. So a lot of it is specific to the individual psychology.

**Sam:** There was that article that came out years ago saying you don't get significantly happier after $70,000 a year. I always thought that was crazy. I do think there's a threshold — like if you're in the $10 to $15 million liquid range, life doesn't change significantly more after that because you'd have to really screw up majorly. But below that threshold, yeah.

**Michael:** The $70,000 is a number that shows up in the literature because below $70,000 every increment that gets you closer to it has dramatic increases — it's basically covering basic needs. A roof, square meals, a car. At $70,000 you can afford most of those things, not in the lap of luxury but you can afford them. Below that it's really tough.

Everything above that — for every level you have, you can just spend more and create the same sense of being overextended if you're not disciplined. It's the rare person who generates excess capital and doesn't change their lifestyle. Those who can keep that gap — not needing what they have — that gives them a cushion. That's rare and remarkable.

**Sam:** Have you met people who've been worth hundreds of millions but still live like they don't?

**Michael:** The most classic example would be Warren Buffett, who still lives in a $30,000 or $50,000 home in Omaha. It's really hard to comment on another person's situation. There's tremendous benefit from the money he's given to charity. But it's one thing to live within your means — that's responsible. It's another thing to be so disconnected between your wealth and what you're living on that there's something going on inside. I'll leave it to the shrinks to figure it out, but it's kind of hard to understand why a person who might be working 60, 70, 80 hours a week — and then they're worth a certain amount but living on a hundredth of it as if they had a hundredth of the wealth. What are they hiding from?

**Michael:** There are as many differences as there are people, but there are some fundamental truths you're pointing at.

When I went to business school at MIT 40-some years ago, if you were doing a marketing exercise they'd say, "Come up with a product you could sell to a thousand people." Today if you go to Stanford to a marketing course, they say, "Come up with a service you could sell to a billion people." That's what Facebook does. Scaling is not just a difference in size — we have a dramatically more frictionless economy. The nature of the internet allows businesses to scale. That's why we have unicorns today but almost never did in the past.

So when you think about life expectations, people who started out 30 or 40 years ago expected to put their nose to the grindstone for 30 years before a payday. Today if you sell, you might be 30 or 35 or 38. You're not retiring, you're going on to the next thing. The biggest difference is this kind of expectation of what a life looks like.

A generation ago, a really successful entrepreneur had one, maybe two serial successes. Today a successful entrepreneur could have three, four, or five — or run multiples at the same time. Elon Musk is not the only person who has multiple businesses. He's certainly the most visible, but it's much more a creature of today.

## Should You Sell Your Business? [00:27:00]

**Sam:** When I was selling, some advisors were like, "Sell it and get paid, you don't have to worry about money anymore." And then another group said, "Man, starting something that works is really really hard. A lot of people sell and want to start again but don't realize how much luck was involved, and it's just actually really really challenging." I've lately fallen into the category of: if you don't have a lot of money, sell. But after that, often try not to sell anything ever — see if you can just run it or hire people, because starting something is such a pain.

When you're talking to your members, which of those categories do you think is more true?

**Michael:** I think you're onto something really important, and it's kind of all of the above.

One of the biggest learnings is that most people who sell their first business have no idea it feels like having the rug pulled out from under you. You see a big dollar amount and you're so focused on the sale that everything else is kind of the shock. The most important one is the momentum — the platform — that you don't even realize how valuable it is.

One of the things we really talk about is: before you sell, you really have to think about not just the pros but the cons. Particularly in a low interest rate environment, when you sell, a typical business might sell for seven or eight times earnings. Let's say a business making $3 million sells for $20 million. You pay taxes, now you have $16 million. If you buy bonds at 2%, you're now making $320,000 on the same capital that was generating $3 million before. You've lost 90% of your earning power. We call that sticker shock.

The vast majority of people who sell their first business go through sticker shock because they haven't thought through that the passive earnings on the profits of the sale will generate dramatically less income than the business itself did. Of course, the positive is that you don't have all the risks of that business anymore — a good sale allows you to take chips off the table, and that has real benefits. But when you hold on to the business, there's no tax liability, and the full value is working to make the business larger.

## Asset Allocation of the Ultra-Wealthy [00:33:00]

**Sam:** Your annual report on asset allocation is awesome. What does a fairly successful TIGER 21 member's portfolio look like?

**Michael:** I want to be clear — we're not investment advisors, but I do track the asset allocation of our members and can simply report on it.

Traditionally, real estate has been king, at about 27 to 28% of assets. That's mostly investment real estate — they either own a building or they're in a limited partnership in a real estate fund of some sort. Not primary residence, generally.

Second would be public equity, about 24 to 26%.

Then private equity — this is unique to the TIGER community — our private equity has for the last couple of years actually been more than public equity. It's now neck and neck, but private equity is around 21 to 24%.

What's remarkable is if you take those three numbers — private equity, public equity, and real estate — it adds up to 70-plus percent. Those are the risk-on assets. So our members are relatively long-term bullish on investments even when they think we're going into a recession.

Fixed income — until recently — has been at a historic low point, about 7%. It was as high as 12 to 14% in prior years. Crypto and gold, creeping up to 1 or 2% each. Cash has stayed relatively fixed over a long period of time, typically fluctuating between 11 and 13%. In March 2020 it spiked to 20%, which statistically is off the charts — that's how concerned our members were.

**Sam:** What do they love about real estate so much?

**Michael:** It's the gift that keeps on giving. First, there are terrific tax attributes — depreciation. When most people think of an investment asset and they buy a company, that company is subject to competitive forces every single day. That's why most operating businesses don't lend themselves to multi-generational families — though of course there are important exceptions.

Real estate is a much more tolerant asset for multi-generational wealth. The joke is, your child can be less than brilliant and still know how to collect the rent. In your own lifestyle, if you own a great piece of real estate, the tenants have to pay the rent even when you're playing golf. But when you're running a complicated technology company, you've got to be out there working every day.

One way to think about it: if you broke the economy into all its sectors and one was real estate and nine were everything else — medical, education, high tech, aerospace — real estate is more different from those other nine than any of the other nine are to each other. Because of this durability, this multi-generational capacity.

We always make a joke — people get a little offended but I think it's a compliment — that real estate has the highest number of "dumb rich people." Not that they don't have intellectual pursuits, but they have a nose for opportunity, a way to sniff out where there's value that most other business owners don't. That said, it's become a little less so in the last generation because as real estate became more securitized, it became more of a Wall Street game.

**Sam:** The strategy my wife and I had was to get somewhat wealthy with tech stuff and pile a lot of it into real estate. With my business, we had to send an email every day, and if Gmail changed it could go out of business. A building I own — I could lose a fair amount of money, but it's still a thing someone will purchase. It's not going to zero. Whereas my company definitely could — if I owned a conference business and a pandemic hit, which I did, it literally made zero dollars.

**Michael:** I think you're making some really excellent points, but if you owned retail or movie theaters or airports in the pandemic, that went to zero too if you had any leverage on it. It's not without risk. But as an asset category it's a long-dated asset.

## The Sticker Shock of Selling [00:42:00]

**Sam:** One more thing about selling versus not selling, because you have a way bigger sample size. Do you regret selling your companies? And — the thing about selling is, let's say you sell a business and make $30 million. You pay taxes, you're left with $20 million. To make $20 million from annual cash flow, your taxes are higher because it's income versus capital gains, and it's just really really hard. Selling a business seems like it's the easier way to accumulate your first bit. Would you agree?

**Michael:** The problem is it's better to think of it as a shift in risk. When you made the decision to sell for $30 million, what you've done is taken a lot of risk off the table. The chance of losing that $30 million goes down radically once you've turned it into cash. But the chance of getting to $60 million might have gone down also, because you lost the engine of growth.

Are you long-term greedy or short-term greedy? There's no question that when you sell your business, more often than not it's not number-dependent, it's risk-dependent. You want to take risk off the table. You can take your chips and diversify over a number of investments. But if the business was a well-functioning, growing business, you've increased the hurdle rate before you can get to that next level of $60 or $80 or $100 million.

**Sam:** Do you regret selling your businesses?

**Michael:** I'm still the chairman and majority owner of TIGER, so I didn't fully sell it. It's run as if I'm a 50/50 partner. I'm 66, and the risk I was most concerned about — because I'm a cancer survivor — is longevity and sustainability. Our board said I was the number one risk. Because I love this business, I said, how do I reduce that risk? First, bring in a co-owner. Second, bring in a world-class CEO. In order to bring in that CEO I needed to bring in a co-owner.

TIGER is immeasurably stronger today from a team and ownership and resilience point of view. It's still my legacy. I'm the founder. It was my idea. But I never went into it for money, and I'm not sure I would have done any better on my own. The diversity of ownership and the addition of a world-class team is just dramatically different than I could have created alone. No regrets.

With my first project, I sold when I was 30. The project sold for over $100 million, and that was in 1987. Life-changing.

**Sam:** That must have been pretty life-changing.

**Michael:** Totally life-changing. But what I remember is not so much the dollar amount. I remember it was heralded as the most successful real estate project in metropolitan history in terms of financial return. But it took me a couple of years to find myself again, because I went from being one of the top 100 developers in the country to — well, selling was actually my partner's idea. The project was my idea, but he never would have gotten into it without me, and I never would have sold it without him. We sold at a point where 99% of what we were worth was tied up in that project. If somebody had gotten hurt in construction, it could have wiped us out.

We closed in January of '87, and in October '87 the market crashed. We had been one of the top 100 developers, and we might have been one of five developers in the whole country that had lots of capital and no buildings weighing us down with problems from the market crash. That gave us an extraordinary opportunity to get back in at the bottom, which I did by creating a company that bought distressed real estate from banks — ultimately close to a billion dollars of assets.

## Surprising Things Rich People Learn [00:49:00]

**Sam:** When I sold, one thing that surprised me was I'd never heard of this thing called an asset-backed loan. Rich people buy a $5 or $10 million home "in cash," but what that often means is they're using an asset-backed loan — if you have a $10 million stock portfolio in a Vanguard index fund, you can borrow 60 to 75% of it at a very low interest rate. What are some other surprising things like that which people discover when they come into money?

**Michael:** I'm sorry, but I would never do that — and most TIGER members wouldn't, for two reasons. One, I don't have any debt and I wouldn't want any. Debt can be very corrosive at exactly the wrong time. The market has just gone down, and for people who are levered they could have been wiped out. If you want to be here for the long term and preserve wealth, you want as little debt as possible.

The other part is that 1% can be illusory because the rate can go up if it's a floating rate. If you have a spike in interest rates and you made assumptions based on borrowing at a low rate, and then it goes up, you can really wreak havoc with your balance sheet.

That said, who are the best managers of debt? Private equity firms. And real estate is the place where most debt is. People who've spent a career managing debt understand its power — they just understand that at some point you want to reduce risk and take chips off the table.

I think the other thing is that not everybody fully appreciates why they became successful. For every person who had a plan, somebody else was lucky — statistically it's just a thing. The number of people who've had two successes is dramatically smaller than those who've had one, and for people to have three or four gets down to maybe one in a thousand entrepreneurs.

Many people systematically overestimate their own skills when they've been successful. After they sell their first successful business, they assume they'll be successful in the next one — and they get their clock handed to them, as I did when I was 30. Very painful lesson. But the most valuable.

I sold this amazing project at 30, then started a business in real estate information — basically what Zillow is today, before there was an internet. I risked somewhere between 20 and 25% of my net worth from the previous sale. That was one of the biggest learnings: when you own a business, if you make an investment and lose money, it gets swept under the carpet because the business itself is profitable. The business covers up losses. So many people who've been successful entrepreneurs don't realize how poorly they are as investors because they've never been battle-tested without the benefit of the company providing a cushion. When you sell, you lose that buffer.

## The Marshmallow Test and Spending Money [00:55:00]

**Sam:** What about spending money? I'm quite frugal — I don't want to buy this or that because it costs too much. But then there are other things, like flying nicely or staying at fancy hotels, where I was like, I don't want to spend money on that, and then I was like, well, actually that makes me happier. I feel like you should spend on those things. Are you familiar with the marshmallow test?

**Michael:** Sure — delayed gratification.

**Sam:** Should I tell it or will you?

**Michael:** It's a very famous test done at Stanford in the '50s or early '60s. They put a table of 12 three- or four-year-olds out, put one marshmallow in front of each kid, and said, "I'm going to step out of the room. If you don't touch the marshmallow while I'm gone, when I come back I'll give you another one." Only one or two of the kids could wait the 20 minutes. Those one or two could handle delayed gratification.

It turns out they tracked many kids who went through this, and the very few who could delay gratification were more successful in school, more successful in life, and more successful in business by various measures over the next 30 to 50 years. It's a remarkable study.

When you're an entrepreneur, you had to delay gratification because you were putting money into your business. You couldn't do all the things others who were making a lot less but didn't have a business were doing. Delayed gratification is at the core of entrepreneurial success.

But when you're a movie star or a rock star or a basketball player, delayed gratification isn't so much at the core of your success. You need to practice and be well — you don't need to scrimp and save.

So when people sell, there's a syndrome where someone has been very successful but has been delaying gratification for so long that they only open up one step at a time. They're still driving a Chevy, wearing a Timex watch — but they go on a great vacation, or they're wearing a really expensive watch. When people sell, they open up slowly. They want to be smart and not waste, and they have to find that new balance.

We have a rule at TIGER I think is the most important rule: the two percent rule. If you're solely living on investments in your portfolio, if you can live on two percent of your assets or less, then you're in a safety zone. Obviously some people have the good fortune to earn more than two or three percent on their assets and can live on a little more. But once you start living on more than two percent, you're actually starting to stress the ability to preserve capital.

If you went to a bunch of 25-year-olds and said, "If you inherited a million dollars, how much could you spend a year and preserve the money?" — you'd be shocked. A lot of kids would say $100,000 or $200,000 a year. The answer is $20,000 a year.

**Sam:** There's this subreddit called Fat FIRE. You know what FIRE is — Financially Independent Retire Early. Fat FIRE is people who want to earn a much larger amount and live somewhat lavishly. On that subreddit the number is like 3%, a little higher than your 2%. There was something called the Trinity Study that said 4%, and I think sometimes that's too high. I kind of buy into the 3% range.

Anyway, have you ever heard of Felix Dennis?

**Michael:** I don't think so.

**Sam:** He's amazing — he's dead now, but he was kind of a combination of Mick Jagger and Richard Branson. A British entrepreneur, flamboyant like Branson but kind of a degenerate — loved drugs, never married, wrote about his escapades. He founded Micro Warehouse and started Maxim Magazine. Made all his money in magazines. He wrote this book called "How to Get Rich" — poorly titled in a way that's embarrassing to recommend, but it's quite a good book.

He said something like: I'm going to die soon, I've got cancer, I'll die with a $600 million net worth. But if I could do it all over again, my goal would have been to make $20 or $30 million by the age of 35 and then not focus on moneymaking ever again — only focus on the people I cared about. But like a punch-drunk boxer, I went back into the ring every time because I was addicted to it, even though it didn't always bring me the most fulfillment and happiness.

**Michael:** I want to share three reactions to that.

First, there was a band leader from the late '60s or early '70s being interviewed a few years ago. The announcer said, "You were the first band to make a million dollars a month. What'd you do with all that money? You guys must be rich." He said, "Well, we spent about 70% on wine, women, and drugs — and wasted all the rest."

**Sam:** The thing with Mick Jagger is what you don't see is he works out six hours a day and eats a food regimen that's second to none.

**Michael:** Even with Richard Branson — whatever your persona is, he knows how to lever his name from a marketing point of view. He puts nothing into a business and others invest around his name. His risk is very low. These are real geniuses at what they do.

## Philanthropy and Meaning [01:05:00]
